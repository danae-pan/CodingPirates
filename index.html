How Human Vision Works

When light hits an object, like a dog, it bounces off the object and travels into our eyes. The light first passes through a clear layer called the cornea, which helps focus it. Next, the light goes through the pupil, which controls how much light enters. The retina at the back of the eye then receives the light, where special sensors called rods and cones detect colors and shapes.

Our brain then works like a puzzle solver, turning the light into information about color, shape, movement, and detail, helping us understand what we're seeing!

How Computer Vision Works

This is how we can see but what about computers? Well, like we use our eyes, computers use digital cameras equipped with sensors to capture light. These sensors, usually CMOS (Complementary Metal Oxide Semi-conductor) or sometimes CCD (Charge-Coupled Device), detect light (photons) and convert it into electrical signals (electrons).

To reproduce human color vision, the acquisition mimics the three kinds of cones by using filtering in front of the sensor. One commonly used filter is the Bayer filter, a mosaic of red, green, and blue filters, which is placed over a square grid of photosensors. Each filter acts like a colored lens, only allowing light of its corresponding wavelength to pass through while blocking the others. This means that each pixel in the sensors detects only one color channel, e.g., a red pixel records the intensity of red light but knows nothing about the green and blue light. But what can we do with this? How would we find the missing colors? Cameras come to rescue, using a process called demosaicing which estimates the missing color values for each pixel by analyzing the surrunding pixels. One simple approach to this is averaging neighboring pixels.
